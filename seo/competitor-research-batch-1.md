# Competitor Research — Batch 1 (May 2026)

Research notes for the 6 competitor comparison blog posts queued for the Blog Post Writer cron. All citations are from the competitor's own homepage / pricing pages, fetched 2026-05-05.

The unifying angle across all six posts is the **production-builder design center model** — Cornerstone's 64 Designer Packages, per-floorplan options (not per-job selections), Structural vs Designer scope separation, Option Classes tied to scope, Area Cost Pricing, and the floor-plan-repeat economy where one plan's options get sold across 100 homes in 5 communities. None of these competitors model a production builder this way.

---

## 1. AccuLynx — https://acculynx.com

AccuLynx positions itself unambiguously as the **#1 roofing software** with "exclusive focus on roofing." Their headline metrics — "9 hours saved per week" and "32% more profit after year 1" — are roofing-contractor metrics, not whole-home metrics. The platform stack is roofing CRM + sales proposals + measurement integrations + supplier direct ordering (ABC Supply, QXO, SRS) + crew labor management + invoicing/financing. Essential plan is $250/month.

**What's missing for whole-home builders:** No floor plans. No design center / selections concept. No options engine. No multi-community model. No takeoff for non-roofing scopes (framing, MEP, drywall, etc.). The whole product is built around a single trade workflow — a roofing contractor wins a deal, measures the roof, orders shingles from ABC Supply, schedules a crew, invoices when done.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** AccuLynx is best-in-class for what it does, but a production home builder running 50+ homes/year across 3 communities with 130+ scope items per house cannot run on it. Cornerstone's Designer Packages, per-floorplan options, structural-vs-designer scope separation, and community-assigned vendor wins are entire dimensions AccuLynx does not have. The post is not "AccuLynx is bad" — it's "AccuLynx is for roofers, Cornerstone is for builders."

## 2. Procore — https://procore.com

Procore markets itself as "The #1 end-to-end construction management solution" serving **General Contractors, Owners, and Specialty Contractors**. The pillar modules are Project Execution, Cost Management, Resource Management, and Project Lifecycle Management. They've launched "Procore AI" for jobsite automation. Pricing is enterprise/quote-based, no published list price, and known to land in the tens of thousands of dollars annually with significant implementation effort.

**What's wrong for residential home builders:** Procore is built for commercial GCs running one $50M school or one $200M hospital. The data model is project-centric, not floorplan-centric. There is no concept of a Designer Package, per-floorplan options, plan repeats, model homes, or community-assigned vendors. No design center. No options pricing engine for buyers. RFIs, submittals, and drawings are first-class objects; Designer Packages and à-la-carte selections are not.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** A residential production builder doing 80 homes/year does not need RFI workflows on a $400k single-family. They need the Magnolia plan set up once with options, sold 100 times across 5 communities, with vendor bids locked after acceptance and a buyer-facing design center. Procore's "for everyone in construction" pitch is its weakness for production residential — too much commercial machinery, no production-residential economy.

## 3. CoConstruct — https://coconstruct.com

CoConstruct's homepage now redirects to a **"CoConstruct + Buildertrend: Better together"** page — they were acquired and customers are being migrated to Buildertrend. Their stated mission was "simplify the business for busy builders" with selections and client communication tools for **custom builders**. Selections are job-by-job — a custom builder picks tile, faucets, paint colors per house with the homeowner.

**What's missing for production builders:** Custom-builder selections ≠ production-builder design center. CoConstruct (and Buildertrend, the acquirer) treat selections as job-level allowances negotiated per homeowner. Cornerstone treats design as **plan-level**: 64 Designer Packages curated across 7 categories, options priced once per floorplan, reused across every home of that plan in every community. CoConstruct has no Designer Package abstraction, no Structural vs Designer scope separation, no Option Classes tied to bid templates, no Area Cost Pricing.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** CoConstruct is the "custom builder picking tile with one homeowner" tool. Cornerstone is the "Magnolia plan repeat, sold 100 times, options auto-locked to a Designer Package" tool. The migration-to-Buildertrend story also adds churn-risk anxiety — builders forced to migrate once are open to migrating again to a platform actually built for their model.

## 4. Contractor Foreman — https://contractorforeman.com

Contractor Foreman markets itself as "The most affordable project management software for contractors" starting at **$49/month**. Audience is "Subcontractors, Trade Contractors, Residential General Contractors, Commercial General Contractors." Modules are Project Management, Financials, People (CRM-lite), Documents (RFIs, submittals, forms), and 50+ integrations (QuickBooks, CompanyCam, Stripe, Zapier, Kreo Takeoff). Basic plan is 1 user.

**What's underpowered for production home builders:** No design center. No options engine. No floorplan model. No Designer Packages. No community/lot/model abstraction. No takeoff (they integrate Kreo, but it's a separate purchase and not residential-floorplan-aware). Pricing structure (user-based seats, $49/month tier with 1 user) signals SMB/sub-contractor positioning, not 50–200 home/year builders.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** Contractor Foreman is a great $49/month tool for a 4-person GC running roofing + remodel + small commercial jobs out of a pickup truck. A production home builder needs floorplan-level option pricing, multi-community vendor assignment, AI takeoff parsing CAD PDFs into 3,284+ parts, and a buyer-facing design center with locked package logic. Cornerstone is a different product category, not a more expensive Contractor Foreman.

## 5. Buildxact — https://buildxact.com

Buildxact is **estimating-first** with a residential-builder/remodeler focus. Pricing is Foundation $199/mo, Pro $399/mo, Master $599/mo (annual). They've launched "Blu" — an AI assistant suite (Assembly Assistant, Estimate Reviewer, Takeoff Assistant, Estimate Generator) — most as paid add-ons ($99–$149/mo each). Integrates with material dealers for live pricing. Job management is in the Pro tier; estimating is the core.

**Where it falls short for production builders:** Buildxact's strength is "build a quote fast for a remodel or one-off custom job." It does takeoff, estimate, quote, then schedule + track. There is no Designer Package model, no per-floorplan options reused across plan repeats, no Structural vs Designer scope split, no community-assigned vendor wins, no buyer-facing design center, no Area Cost Pricing for community-level extras. Blu AI is good for estimating, but Cornerstone has 37+ AI agents covering takeoff, scheduling, bid import, MLS listings, and more — with all of them included, not as $99/mo add-ons.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** Buildxact is the "fast quote for the next remodel job" tool. Cornerstone is the "set up Magnolia plan once, sell 100 of them across 5 communities with locked Designer Packages" tool. The pricing comparison also matters — Buildxact's full-AI Master plan is $599/mo + add-ons, vs Cornerstone's bundled approach.

## 6. JobNimbus — https://jobnimbus.com

JobNimbus brands itself as "The #1 Roofing CRM & Project Management Software" trusted by 6,000+ roofing businesses. Stack is roofing-specific: lead organization → sales automation → project boards → billing/financing (JobNimbus Payments) + SumoQuote proposals + QuickBooks/QXO/CompanyCam integrations. Mobile-first, contractor-friendly, customizable workflows. Strong pitch for roofing/exterior contractors.

**What's missing for whole-home builders:** Same gap as AccuLynx — JobNimbus is a roofing/exterior CRM, not a home-builder PM. No floorplans. No design center. No Designer Packages. No options pricing. No community-lot-model abstraction. No multi-trade scope item architecture. The "production" they reference is roofing crew production, not home production. Estimating is via SumoQuote or QXO material lists — not a residential takeoff parsing 130+ scopes from a CAD PDF.

**Cornerstone differentiation angle:** JobNimbus is excellent for a roofer or exterior contractor scaling from $1M to $15M (they cite this case study). It is the wrong tool for a 50-home/year production builder. Cornerstone's design center alone — 64 Designer Packages, per-floorplan options, structural-vs-designer scope separation — is an entire product layer JobNimbus has no equivalent for. The post should be respectful of what JobNimbus does well and direct about the segment mismatch.

---

## Cross-cutting observations

1. **The roofing-CRM cluster (AccuLynx, JobNimbus)** is huge but solves a different problem. Marketing posts should be respectful, segment-mismatch-focused, not feature-shaming.
2. **The custom-builder cluster (CoConstruct/Buildertrend, JobTread already covered)** has selections but only at the job level. The Designer Package + per-floorplan options angle is the strongest differentiator.
3. **The estimating cluster (Buildxact)** is rapidly bolting AI onto the legacy estimating model, but as paid add-ons. Cornerstone's bundled-AI + design-center + multi-community model is a different category.
4. **The commercial-enterprise cluster (Procore)** has deep machinery for the wrong workflow. Marketing should highlight that production residential is not "small commercial."
5. **The cheap GC cluster (Contractor Foreman)** is a price competitor that disqualifies on data model, not on price alone.
